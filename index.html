Overview

An Invitation Through Light

Chosen location: End of the corridor that connects the Industrial Design part of the building with the Culture Technology part of the building.

Protocol: A lot of ID students either believe that the building ends with that corridor or are unaware of the other part of the building, so they never actually go there.

Minimal intervention implemented: Colored panels applied on the window creating a "stained glass" decoration, projecting colorful eye-catching sunrays onto the wall. This is intended to grab the attention of anyone coming in/out of the elevator, making them want to see the design closer, hence discovering the other side of the building and discovering the corridor itself too.

Spatial Manifestation & Analysis

Deployment vs. Protocol Logic

Figures 01–02: Installation field outputs. Click images to inspect structural alignment and illumination density up close.
What did you want to change, reveal, or make visible?
To disrupt structural unawareness. The gesture utilizes shifting sunlight matrices to physically cast colored landmarks onto unlit concrete paths, transforming a dead boundary into an active sensory trigger.
Why this addition, and not something else?
Unlike an explicit orientation map or static floor sign that students filter out as environmental noise, ambient light filters require zero infrastructure and dynamically alter the structural scale across distinct transit hours.
What impact did it bring?
Passersby explicitly decelerated on transit tracks. The unexpected visibility of chromatic rays hitting the walls prompted direct environmental discovery, breaking default habits and re-orienting circulation beyond the ID border.
Did it work as intended?
Not quite, I expected the colors to projection the wall, making it visible from when exit the elevator. However, I believe this is timing issue, I believe at around 1-2pm on a sunny day it should work as intended, though I wasn’t lucky to capture that on camera.
